What is an Option?
An option is a real path available to you right now — not a wish, not a fantasy. A genuine road you could actually take. The lab asks you to name three, because one isn't a real choice, two creates a false either/or, and three forces honest thinking about what is actually in front of you.
What are Considerations?
For each option you identify up to five things that speak for it — and five that speak against it. Across three options that's up to 30 considerations in total. Each one gets a rating from 1 to 5. The more honest you are about what actually influences you — including the uncomfortable things — the more useful the map becomes.
What are the 3 Analysis Levels?
Once your considerations are rated, the lab runs three levels of analysis. Level I ranks your options by total score. Level II flags your critical signals — anything rated ±5 that demands closer attention. Level III counts the full complexity of your decision and checks whether you have been fully honest with yourself.
What does ±5 mean?
Every consideration gets a rating. A 1 means it is a mild factor — worth noting but not decisive. A 5 means it matters deeply — a critical signal that speaks louder than any average. The scale runs from +5 for the strongest pros to −5 for the most serious cons. Rate honestly, not strategically. Your gut knows the difference.

Name the Situation

Start by simply naming what you are facing — the problem, challenge, dilemma, conflict, or issue. Raw and honest. Just get it out.

Example I am 15 and already designing hats and beanies. I can start selling now using print-on-demand, but it is expensive and risky. If I wait, I could save money and maybe do it better later. Reframed as a point of decision: I am at a moment of choice about whether to start building my clothing brand now or wait and save. Guiding values: Learning, financial security, not wasting money Write your situation as it actually is — messy and unresolved is fine. Then reframe it as a fork in the road.
Mentor Note Don't edit yourself here. Write it as you would tell a trusted friend — messy, uncertain, unresolved. That honesty is what makes the next step possible.
Why this matters This single act — reframing your situation as a point of decision — is the Dawning of Agency. You are not stuck — you are at a fork in the road.
The Dawning of Agency is the moment you realise you are a chooser — not a passenger. The situation has not changed. Your relationship to it has.
Two people can be in the same situation and live completely different realities. The problem hasn't changed. But your relationship to it just did.
Your Guiding Values A choice can only align with you if you know what matters to you. Name what actually matters — not what should matter or what sounds good. Look at where your time and money go — that is often more honest than what you say. These values will anchor your ratings and make the alignment check at the end more meaningful.
Where did your energy actually go last year — not where you intended it to go?
When have you felt most like yourself — what were you doing, and who were you with?
What would you refuse to compromise even if it cost you something real?
What do you want to be true about how you lived this year?
Sit with these quietly. Then name your guiding values above.
🔵 Option A
Name your first option

An option is a real path you could actually take — not a wish or a fantasy. Given where you are right now, what is one genuine road available to you?

Example — Option A Start now with print-on-demand — list one hat design this month and see if anyone buys it. Be specific. A good option names what you would actually do, not just a general direction.
What makes a good option? It must be real and genuinely available to you right now. "Win the lottery" is not an option. "Do nothing" is — and it too has consequences.
🔴 Option B
Name your second option

This should be a genuinely different path — not a minor variation of Option A. A real second road that leads somewhere different.

Example — Option B Wait 6–12 months, save money, then launch properly with bulk inventory and better margins. Option B should lead somewhere genuinely different — not just a slower version of Option A.
🟢 Option C
Name your third option

Three options force real thinking. Consider the one you have been quietly avoiding naming — the path that feels too risky, too unconventional, or too honest to say out loud.

Example — Option C Start with just one design as a low-cost test — spend under $50, learn what I can, and decide from there. This is often the option people skip. It is sometimes the most honest one — and the most useful to name.
🔵 Option A — Pros & Cons
Map the pros and cons

Write what genuinely influences you about this option — what speaks for it, and what speaks against it. Rate each from 1 (mild) to 5 (critical). Be honest, especially about the uncomfortable ones.

Example — Option A pros and cons ✚ Pro: Start learning business skills immediately +4 ✚ Pro: People already like my hat designs +3 ✖ Con: Might lose money if designs do not sell −4 ✖ Con: High costs per product −3 Rate 5 only if this consideration alone could change your decision. Rate 1 if it barely matters.
🔴 Option B — Pros & Cons
Map the pros and cons

What speaks for this option — and what speaks against it? Same honest treatment as Option A. The comparison only works if all three are equally truthful.

Example — Option B pros and cons ✚ Pro: Higher profit margins with bulk ordering later +4 ✚ Pro: Less financial stress while saving +3 ✖ Con: Might lose motivation over 6–12 months −4 ✖ Con: No real-world experience during waiting period −4 Give Option B the same honest treatment as Option A — do not go easier on it or harder on it.
🟢 Option C — Pros & Cons
Map the pros and cons

Last one. Be as honest here as you were in the first two — especially if this is the option you've been quietly avoiding naming.

Example — Option C pros and cons ✚ Pro: Low financial risk — test with minimal spend +4 ✚ Pro: Generates real data before committing fully +5 ✖ Con: Limited scale — can only test one design −2 Sometimes the most honest pros and cons belong to the option you almost did not name.

Knowing Your Self is a verb, not a noun — tap any aspect below for a brief reflection to help you. What are the six aspects?
The six aspects of Knowing Your Self: Needs — what you require to flourish. Beliefs — what you hold to be true. Values — what matters when it costs something. Interests — what sustains your attention. Intentions — what you are actually trying to bring about. Strengths — what you are genuinely good at.
Needs ▾

Beliefs ▾

Values ▾
A value is what matters when it costs something. Look at where your time and money have actually gone — that is often more honest than what you say you value.
Interests ▾
An interest is what sustains your attention without effort. Which option connects to something you would pursue even if nobody was watching?
Intentions ▾
An intention is what you are actually trying to bring about — not what you say, but what your choices show. There are no wrong intentions here. Just honest ones.
Strengths ▾
A strength is what you are genuinely capable of at your best. Which option gives you the best chance to act from your actual strengths — not the ones you wish you had?

The C³ Glossary
Key terms from the C³ framework — explained simply.
Field of Influence
The bigger forces shaping your life that you did not choose — your family, your culture, your economic situation, the moment in history you were born into. These forces shape what feels possible before you even begin choosing. No choice happens in a vacuum.
Circumstance
Not just what is happening — but how you are actually experiencing it right now. Two people can be in the same situation and have completely different circumstances, because circumstance is not just observed. It is lived.
Dawning of Agency
The moment you realise you are making a choice — not just having something happen to you. This is the shift from feeling like a passenger to knowing you are at the wheel. It does not change the situation. It changes your relationship to it.
Knowing Your Self
Understanding six things about yourself: what you need to flourish, what you believe, what you actually value, what genuinely interests you, what you are trying to bring about, and what you are good at. A choice can only truly fit you if you know who you are.
Guiding Values
What you actually care about — shown by how you spend your time and money, not just what you say. Your real values are visible in your choices before you name them. This framework asks you to name them honestly before you start mapping options.
Alignment
When a choice fits who you actually are — your needs, beliefs, values, interests, intentions, and strengths. The highest-scoring option is not automatically the most aligned one. Alignment asks: does this feel right for me — not just logical?
Wise Counsel
The people whose honest perspective you trust enough to seek before a big decision. Not people who will just agree with you — people who will tell you what they actually think. Choosing who you let into your decisions is itself one of the most important choices you make.
Consequence
What happens after a choice — including what it teaches you about yourself. Consequences are not punishments or rewards. They are information. When you face them honestly, you learn. When you avoid them, they tend to grow.
The Ripple
The way your choices affect other people's circumstances — often without you seeing it. No choice is ever purely private. What you decide today becomes part of the situation someone else is navigating tomorrow.
Choice Precedent
The quiet pattern your past choices have set. Every significant choice shapes what feels available next time. Over time, your choices add up to a direction — whether you intended it or not.
Satisficing
Choosing something good enough — given your values and circumstances — rather than waiting for the perfect option that may never arrive. A good enough choice made honestly and in time is often wiser than a perfect choice made too late.
The C³ Formula
C³ = C : C → C
Circumstance : Choice → Consequence. You meet a situation. You make a choice. That choice creates a consequence — which becomes part of someone else's circumstance. The loop never stops.
