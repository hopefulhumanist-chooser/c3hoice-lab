Before the Framework — A Starting Point
Have You Met Your Choices Yet?
Not everyone arrives at the moment of recognising their own freedom through a single dramatic moment. Some people need to look back first — to see the pattern of choices already made before they can own the ones ahead. If you have not yet experienced a clear moment of awakening to your own agency, begin with your Choice History. Map how you have spent your time and money. Chronicle the significant choices of the last five years. Name the choice precedents that have been forming without your awareness. That mapping is not a detour — it is the path. The framework below will make far more sense once you have seen the evidence that you have always been choosing.
+
How you actually spend time and money reveals your values more honestly than anything you would say if asked directly
−
Choices made without self-awareness become precedents — patterns that quietly narrow what feels possible next
Every choice is a self-portrait. You do not need to have named your values to have been living by them. Look at where your time and money have actually gone — that is the most honest mirror available.

The Subject of This Entire Framework
The Chooser
A chooser is not simply someone who picks from a menu. A chooser is the active centre of a life — shaped by limits you did not choose, pressures you did not invite, and a freedom you cannot escape.
The chooser is the individual who, in each moment, must act within limits, interpret abundance, commit without certainty, assume responsibility, confront the absurd, move between safety and growth, and live deliberately according to conscience.
· Bounded by cognitive and informational limits
· Burdened by excessive options
· Empowered by will and commitment
· Responsible for self-authorship
· Challenged by an indifferent world
· Motivated by needs and potential
· Guided by conscience and simplicity
Thus: the chooser is the evolving self who must continually decide who to become, what to value, and how to act — despite uncertainty, pressure, and constraint. And the choices that matter most are not the ones made alone — they are the ones made in the direction of others.

The Soil — Where Everything Begins
The Field of Influence
The Field of Influence is third-person and expansive. It exists whether anyone is looking at it or not — the larger web of cultural, relational, historical, and environmental forces that determines what circumstances are even possible for anyone standing in it. You did not choose your family, your culture, or the moment in history you arrived into. These are the situation cards already on the table before you drew your first breath.
The Field is not something you experience directly — it is the larger reality that determines what experiences are even possible for you. Circumstance is how the Field arrives in your life. The Field of Influence is the connective tissue between individual agency and collective reality.
+
Your field can be intentionally cultivated — every choice you make ripples back into it
−
A neglected field quietly narrows what circumstances are possible for those around you

C¹ — Circumstance
Circumstance
Circumstance is first-person and situated. It is the world as experienced by a particular consciousness — the specific conditions, pressures, possibilities, and constraints that you face at the moment of choice. It is inherently subjective and local. Two people standing in the same room can have entirely different circumstances, because circumstance is not observed — it is lived. Your circumstances define the real array of options on your canvas right now.
+
Honest awareness of your circumstances reveals real options you might otherwise miss
−
Ignoring circumstance means choosing blind — inside a fog of your own assumptions

The Inner Canvas
Knowing Your Self
Alignment between a choice and your inner self is only possible if you know what your inner self actually is. Congruence is not a feeling — it is a reckoning. These are the six aspects of self that a truly aligned choice must account for.
💧
Needs — what you genuinely require to function and flourish
🧭
Beliefs — what you hold to be true about yourself and the world
⚖️
Values — what matters most to you when it costs something
🔥
Interests — what genuinely draws and sustains your attention
🎯
Intentions — what you are actually trying to bring about
💪
Strengths — what you are genuinely capable of at your best
+
A choice that aligns with all six aspects carries the full weight of who you are
−
A choice made without self-knowledge is alignment by accident rather than by design
The Revelatory Nature of Choice Choices reveal values before they are consciously named. C³ — The Inevitable Echo
Consequence
Consequence is not something you direct. It is the inevitable product of choosing — it flows from your choice the way a ripple flows from a stone. You do not control where the water goes. What you can control is this: whether you accept it honestly, learn from it, and let it make you wiser.
We are challenged not to direct consequences, but to accept them — and in that acceptance, find our greatest freedom.
+
Accepted consequences become the lessons that strengthen your next choice
−
Denied consequences do not disappear — they compound and find you later

The Ripple — The Living Loop
Your Choices Become Others' Circumstances
No choice is ever truly private. Consequences flow back into the Field of Influence — and there they become new circumstances for other souls. The Field shaped your Circumstance. Your Choice produced a Consequence. That Consequence re-enters the Field. And the Field becomes someone else's Circumstance — the ground from which their next choice is made. The loop is living and continuous. You are constantly editing someone else's canvas whether you know it or not.

The Anchor — Not Optional
Wise Counsel
In an absurd world, connection is not weakness — it is one of the wisest choices you can make. Your wise counsel aren't outside your field. They live within it. They expand your real array of options, help you read your circumstances more honestly, and shape better choices through the truth they're willing to tell you.

Choosing your people is one of the highest-order choices you will ever make. It is how you make meaning — and how you refuse to navigate the absurd alone.
+
Wise counsel illuminates blind spots before they become consequences
−
Poor counsel — or none at all — leaves you choosing in the dark
Shoulders We Stand On
Seven thinkers whose ideas about choice inform this framework
Jean-Paul Sartre
Choice as Radical Freedom
"Man is condemned to be free; because once thrown into the world, he is responsible for everything he does."
L'Existentialisme est un humanisme, 1946
C³ connection: The Field of Influence as thrownness. The Dawning of Agency as the recognition of radical freedom.
Albert Camus
Choice as Revolt
"It happens that the stage-sets collapse. Rising, tram, four hours in the office or factory, meal, tram, four hours of work, meal, sleep and Monday, Tuesday, Wednesday, Thursday, Friday and Saturday according to the same rhythm — this path is easily followed most of the time. But one day the ‘why’ arises and everything begins in that weariness tinged with amazement. ‘Begins’ — this is important."
The Myth of Sisyphus, p. 19, Penguin Modern Classics
"At any street corner the feeling of absurdity can strike any man in the face. As it is, in its distressing nudity, in its light without effulgence, it is elusive."
The Myth of Sisyphus, p. 17, Penguin Modern Classics
C³ connection: The first quote is the Dawning of Agency in Camus’s language — the moment the mechanical life cracks open and the ‘why’ arises. The second names the feeling that precedes it. Together they describe what the C³ framework was built to meet.
Abraham Maslow
Choice as Growth
"In each moment, we choose between safety and risk, between stepping forward into growth or back into security."
The Farther Reaches of Human Nature, Viking Press, 1971
"We have come to the point in biological history where we are now responsible for our own evolution… We have become self-evolvers. Evolution means selecting and therefore choosing and deciding, and this means valuing."
The Farther Reaches of Human Nature, Viking Press, 1971
C³ connection: The Fork. Every significant choice is a choice between who you are and who you are becoming. At the species level, Maslow names choice itself as the mechanism of human evolution — to choose is to value, and to value is to become.
William James
Choice as World-Making
"My first act of free will shall be to believe in free will."
Diary entry, April 30, 1870. Quoted in Ralph Barton Perry, The Thought and Character of William James, vol. 1, p. 323
C³ connection: Pragmatic truth — truth found where feeling, utility, scrutiny, and convergence agree. We often must choose before all evidence is in.
Herbert Simon
Choice as Bounded Rationality
"At any moment there are a multitude of alternative (physically) possible actions, any one of which a given individual may undertake; by some process these numerous alternatives are narrowed down to that one which is in fact acted out."
Administrative Behavior, p. 3 (4th ed., Free Press, 1997; originally published 1947)
C³ connection: The Myth of the Golden Choice. We do not optimise — we choose the best available option given our circumstances, values, and limits.
Barry Schwartz
Choice as Overload
"We must decide, individually, when choice really matters and focus our energies there, even if it means letting many other opportunities pass us by. The choice of when to be a chooser may be the most important choice we have to make."
The Paradox of Choice: Why More Is Less, p. 104, HarperCollins, 2004
C³ connection: Why the framework limits options to three. More choices do not always produce more freedom — they often produce paralysis and regret.
Henry David Thoreau
Choice as Deliberate Living
"I went to the woods because I wished to live deliberately."
Walden, 1854
C³ connection: Integration and Coming Home. The goal is not perfect choices — it is a deliberately chosen life, lived with awareness of what each choice reveals.
May the choices you make be the wisest ones possible in the moments you meet them.
